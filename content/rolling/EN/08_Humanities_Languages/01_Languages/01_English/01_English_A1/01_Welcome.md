# Welcome to English A1

This track is written for learners who are **starting from zero or near zero** in English. The **CEFR A1** level means you can manage **short, predictable exchanges**: greetings, numbers, simple present routines, basic questions in a city, and very short descriptions of people and places. The goal is **not** to sound like a native speaker yet; it is to **communicate** and **understand** in everyday situations when people speak slowly and help you.

These lessons read like **book chapters**: explanation → tables → model dialogues → common mistakes → lab → quiz. Work through them in order the first time; then revisit with **spaced repetition**.

@section: 1. What you should be able to do at A1

With support from your interlocutor (repetition, slower speech), you should be able to:

1. Greet and say goodbye appropriately (*Hello*, *Good morning*, *See you later*).
2. Introduce yourself and others (*My name is…*, *This is my colleague*).
3. Talk about nationality, city, and languages (*I’m from…*, *I speak…*).
4. Use numbers for phones, prices, floors, and age with **correct English patterns** (not word‑for‑word from your first language).
5. Use the **present simple** for routines: affirmative, negative, and questions (*I work*, *She doesn’t live here*, *Do you like tea?*).
6. Ask for basic information (*Where is…?*, *How much is…?*, *What time…?*).

The hard part is not “knowing words”; it is **automation**—saying chunks without mentally translating every word.

@section: 2. UK vs US English (don’t stress yet)

At A1 you do **not** need to pick a lifelong dialect. Just notice common differences:

| Area | UK (roughly) | US (roughly) |
|------|----------------|---------------|
| Lexicon | *flat*, *lift*, *rubbish* | *apartment*, *elevator*, *trash* |
| Spelling | *colour*, *centre* | *color*, *center* |

Lessons use a **neutral international** default unless contrast teaches something useful.

@section: 3. Pronunciation priorities

English spelling and pronunciation diverge. Focus early on:

1. **Weak vowels** (*schwa*) in unstressed syllables (*about*, *banana*).
2. **Short vs long vowels** (*ship* vs *sheep*).
3. **Word stress** (wrong stress makes words hard to recognize).
4. **Contractions** in listening (*I’m*, *don’t*, *what’s*).

Listen **and** repeat—shadow short clips daily.

@section: 4. Grammar backbone for this course

You will need:

* Personal and **possessive** pronouns (*my/your/his/her/our/their*).
* ***To be*** for identity, state, and location.
* **Present simple** with *do/does* and **third person ‑s**.
* Basic **articles** (*a/an/the*).
* **Prepositions** of time and place in short phrases.

Grammar is taught **in use**: usable sentences first, rule labels second.

@section: 5. Vocabulary strategy

Avoid huge decontextualized lists. Prefer:

1. **Topics** each week: home, food, transport, work.
2. **Collocations**: *have breakfast*, *take the bus* (not only *breakfast*, *bus*).
3. **Spaced review** at days 2, 7, and 14.

@section: 6. Suggested 12‑week study map

* **Weeks 1–2:** greetings, alphabet/spelling, basic numbers.
* **Weeks 3–4:** family, objects, colors; *there is/are* and *have* (light touch).
* **Weeks 5–6:** daily routines; frequency adverbs.
* **Weeks 7–8:** food and drink; prices in a café.
* **Weeks 9–10:** city, directions, transport.
* **Weeks 11–12:** integrated review; record short dialogues.

Double the timeline if you only have a few hours per week—**consistency beats intensity**.

@section: 7. Typical mistakes (preview)

* Forgetting **third person ‑s** (*He work*).
* **Literal translation** from your L1 for age (*I have twenty years* → *I’m twenty* / *I’m twenty years old*).
* Using *Good night* as a **morning** greeting.
* Over‑answering *How are you?* in a casual service context.

@section: 8. How to use Arborito like a textbook

1. Read dialogues **aloud** twice (slow, then fluent).
2. **Swap** one word in a model sentence (*I live in Madrid* → *I live in Valencia*).
3. **Record** 60–90 seconds after each lesson.
4. Write **three personal sentences** per lesson and review them weekly.

@section: 9. Survival phrases (use inside real sentences)

| Idea | Phrase |
|------|--------|
| Please / Thanks | *Please* / *Thank you* |
| Sorry | *Sorry* / *Excuse me* |
| I don’t understand | *I don’t understand* |
| Repeat | *Can you repeat that, please?* |
| Slowly | *More slowly, please* |
