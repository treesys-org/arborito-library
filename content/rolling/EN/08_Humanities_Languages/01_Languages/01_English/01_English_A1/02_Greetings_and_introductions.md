# Greetings, farewells, and introductions

This chapter trains **high‑frequency chunks** for real life: entering a shop, joining a video call, meeting someone at work, saying goodbye politely. You are not aiming for a speech; you are aiming for **appropriate**, **clear**, **automatic** phrases.

@section: 1. Time‑of‑day greetings and register

### 1.1 Common greetings

| Expression | Typical use | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| *Good morning* | Morning until ~noon (flexible) | Workplace‑friendly. |
| *Good afternoon* | After noon into afternoon | Avoid at midnight. |
| *Good evening* | Late afternoon/evening | Also entering restaurants/events. |
| *Hello* | Neutral, most times | Phone and face‑to‑face. |
| *Hi* | Informal | Friends/peers. |
| *Hey* | Very informal | Use carefully at A1. |

**Important:** *Good night* is usually **farewell** or a bedtime wish—not a 9am office greeting.

### 1.2 First meeting vs people you know

* First time: *Nice to meet you.* → *Nice to meet you too.*
* Known people: *Nice to see you again.* / *How are you?*

@section: 2. Names: asking and answering

### 2.1 Questions

| Question | Register |
|----------|----------|
| *What’s your name?* | Neutral, very common. |
| *May I have your name?* | More formal (services). |
| *Could you tell me your name, please?* | Polite. |

### 2.2 Answers

* *My name is Ana García.*
* *I’m Ana.*
* *This is my colleague, Tom.* (introducing someone else)

### 2.3 Spelling

* *How do you spell that?*
* *It’s A‑N‑A.*

Practice the **alphabet** until letters like *G/J* and *B/V* are clean for listeners.

@section: 3. Introducing another person

Patterns:

1. *This is Carlos. He’s our engineer.*
2. *I’d like you to meet Dr. Smith.*
3. *Everyone, this is Laura from design.*

Model dialogue:

> **A:** Sarah, **this is** James.  
> **B:** Hi, James. **Nice to meet you.**  
> **C:** **Nice to meet you too.** How are you?  
> **B:** I’m fine, thanks.

@section: 4. *How are you?* as a ritual

Common short answers:

* *I’m fine, thanks. And you?*
* *I’m good, thanks. How about you?*
* *Not bad, thanks.*

In quick service small talk, long life stories sound odd; with friends, longer answers are natural.

@section: 5. Farewells

| Expression | Feel |
|-----------|------|
| *Goodbye* | Clear, neutral. |
| *Bye* / *Bye-bye* | Informal. |
| *See you later* | Expect another meeting. |
| *See you soon* | Warm. |
| *Have a nice day* | Shops; reply *You too.* |
| *Take care* | Warm/closing. |
| *Have a good weekend* | Friday/Saturday vibe. |

@section: 6. Applied pronunciation

1. **Schwa** in weak syllables (*about*, *today*).
2. **Contractions** *I’m*, *what’s*, *it’s* in natural speech.
3. **Linking** between words (*an apple*, *turn it on*).

Shadow a native clip **10 times**, then record yourself.

@section: 7. Typical learner mistakes

* *I’m agree* → **I agree** (no *am*).
* *Good night* when you mean *Good morning*.
* A 5‑minute monologue answering *How are you?* from a cashier.

@section: 8. Lab

1. **Shadowing** 3 minutes of subtitled dialogue; imitate rhythm.
2. Write three mini‑dialogues: clothes shop, first work meeting, text to a friend.
3. Record 90 seconds: time‑appropriate greeting + name + *nice to meet you* + farewell.

@section: 9. Summary

You now have **situational greetings**, **names**, **third‑party intros**, **How are you** rituals, and **farewells**. Next: **numbers and time**, which appear inside almost all these situations.

@section: 10. Short professional messages

In chats, *Hi [Name],* works; in email, *Dear [Name],* is safer formal. Close with *Best regards* or *Thanks,* as appropriate. Capitalize *Good morning,* at the start.

@section: 11. Chunks to automate (say each 20 times)

* *Nice to meet you too.*
* *This is my friend …*
* *How are you? — I’m fine, thanks. And you?*

@section: 12. Hotel elevator micro‑dialogue

> **A:** Good morning!  
> **B:** Morning! **Are you** here for the conference?  
> **A:** Yes, **I am**. **My name’s** Leo.  
> **B:** **Nice to meet you**, Leo. **I’m** Maya.
