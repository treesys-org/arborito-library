# Welcome to German A1

**Standard German (*Hochdeutsch*)** is what most learners study first. Germany, Austria, and Switzerland all have strong dialects, but classrooms, news, and many services use a **shared standard** you can rely on at A1.

This course is written like a **textbook**: each chapter has tables, dialogues, common mistakes, labs, and a quiz. Move forward in order the first time; then **review** with spaced repetition.

@section: 1. What A1 means for German

At A1 you can:

* Interact if the other person is willing to repeat or rephrase.
* Produce short phrases for immediate needs (*Ich möchte Wasser*, *Wo ist die U-Bahn?*).
* Recognize very frequent words on signs and simple menus.

German has more **morphology** than Spanish or English: **gender**, **case**, and **verb position** matter early. A1 only needs a **small slice** of those systems—but you cannot ignore them.

@section: 2. Sounds and spelling that pay off early

German uses **ä, ö, ü** and **ß**. Consonant clusters like **sch** and rules for **sp-/st-** at syllable onset are part of the “sound identity” of German.

Daily practice:

1. Listen + repeat short audio.
2. Practice **minimal pairs** (*Bier* vs *vier*).
3. Learn **stress**: German word stress is rule‑guided enough to study systematically.

@section: 3. Gender and articles

Nouns come with gender: **masculine / feminine / neuter**. Learn **noun + article** as a unit (*die Tür*, not *Tür* alone).

Articles in nominative singular: ***der, die, das***. Plural article: ***die*** for all genders.

@section: 4. Verb position (main clauses)

In neutral **main** statements, the **finite verb is second**:

* *Ich **lerne** Deutsch.*

Yes/No questions put the **verb first**:

* ***Kommst** du aus Spanien?*

Wh‑ questions put the **question word first**, verb second:

* ***Wo** wohnst du?*

This is different from English and Spanish—train with **many short sentences**.

@section: 5. *du* vs *Sie*

* ***Sie*** (capitalized) = formal “you” (strangers, service, professional settings).
* ***du*** = informal—use when appropriate.

In shops and offices, stay with ***Sie*** until invited to *du*.

@section: 6. Vocabulary strategy

* Learn by **situations** (airport, home, university).
* Learn **collocations** (*einen Kaffee trinken*).
* Avoid giant lists without sentences.

@section: 7. Suggested three‑month map

* **Month 1:** sounds; greetings; numbers; pronouns; *sein* and *haben*; first nouns with gender.
* **Month 2:** present tense regular verbs; basic questions; accusative objects in simple patterns; food/drink.
* **Month 3:** city/mobility vocabulary; time phrases; integrated review + short recordings.

Stretch the calendar if you study fewer hours—**regularity** beats cramming.

@section: 8. False friends (preview)

* *bekommen* ≈ receive/get (not “become” like *werden*).
* *Gift* = **poison** (dangerous friend!).
* *das Lager* can be camp/warehouse depending on context.
