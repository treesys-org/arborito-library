# IPv4: addressing that works in exams and production

**IPv4** still dominates many LANs and WANs. Mastering **masks**, **CIDR**, **default routes**, and **NAT** (later) is mandatory. This lesson reviews **subnetting**, **broadcast**, and **ARP** as prerequisites before routing.

@section: Notation and prefixes

`192.0.2.10/24` means the first **24 bits** are the network prefix; **8 bits** remain for hosts → 2⁸−2 usable addresses in classic subnets (reserving network and broadcast where applicable).

**CIDR** replaces dotted masks only; learn quick binary conversion for /27, /26, etc.

@section: Special addresses

* **RFC1918:** `10/8`, `172.16/12`, `192.168/16` for private use.
* **Loopback:** `127.0.0.0/8` (usually `127.0.0.1`).
* **APIPA link-local:** `169.254.0.0/16` (no DHCP).

**Multicast:** 224/4; **broadcast** usage varies by context.

@section: Default gateway

The **default gateway** is the next L3 hop toward other subnets. **Local ping** works but **not** the Internet without a route or gateway.

**ICMP redirects** can appear in odd topologies; understand security implications.

@section: ARP

**ARP** answers “which MAC belongs to this IP on my segment?” ARP failures → L3 “exists” but frames do not leave.

**Gratuitous ARP** announces presence (useful and abusable).

@section: Exhaustion and VLSM

**VLSM** allows different subnet sizes under a larger block. Plan **summarization** in routing to shrink tables.

**DHCP** handles dynamic allocation; **reservations** for critical servers/printers.

@section: Common mistakes

* **Wrong host mask** → reaches wrong gateway.
* **Duplicate IP** → flaky behavior.
* **/31** point-to-point links RFC 3021; older stacks may not behave identically.

@section: Suggested lab

1. Manually compute a `/27` subnet inside `10.10.0.0/16` and pick a usable gateway.
2. On Linux add a temporary static route to another subnet and verify with `ip route get`.
3. Watch the ARP table with `ip neigh` during pings.
